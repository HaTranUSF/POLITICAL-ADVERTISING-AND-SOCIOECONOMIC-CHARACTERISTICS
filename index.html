01 / Research

Two Questions. One Dataset.

01

Does political ad spending vary across U.S. states, and how did those patterns change between 2020 and 2024?

We mapped Google political ad spend state by state for both presidential election cycles, then normalized by voter-age population to make cross-state comparisons fair. The goal was to find which states drove the most change and whether spending tracked with competitive races.

02

Are states with lower median income or higher educational attainment targeted with more political ad spending?

Campaigns set budgets strategically. We tested whether socioeconomic characteristics, specifically median household income, poverty rate, and educational attainment, predict where Google political dollars flow, or whether electoral math overrides demographics entirely.

02 / Findings

What the Data Said

🗺️

Swing States Dominate

Political ad spending is concentrated in battleground states regardless of population size. Iowa and Montana led on per-capita spend in 2020 due to competitive Senate races. By 2024, the concentration shifted to presidential swing states.

📈

Massive 2024 Escalation

National totals stayed stable, but individual battleground states saw huge jumps. Pennsylvania averaged $37.8M in 2024, up nearly 7x from 2020. Michigan hit $21M and Wisconsin $9.4M. Safe states barely moved.

💰

Income: No Simple Story

Lower median income does not predict higher spending. High-income swing states like Pennsylvania and Michigan received massive budgets because of their electoral importance. Strategy outweighed demographics.

🎓

Education Correlates Weakly

Higher bachelor's degree attainment shows a moderate association with Democratic-leaning states overall. But within swing states, educational profiles are too mixed to predict ad allocation. Wisconsin and Michigan have average attainment yet top spending.

🔵🔴

The Party Flip: 2020 to 2024

In 2020, 26 states voted Democratic vs. 25 Republican. By 2024, that flipped to 31 states voting Republican, with Arizona, Georgia, and Nevada all switching. Ad budgets followed these shifts closely.

⚖️

Per Capita Changes Everything

Raw spend is misleading. California had high absolute dollars but low per capita due to its size. Iowa and Montana ranked highest per capita in 2020, showing how Senate races pull resources into small-population states.

03 / Data Sources

Where the Data Came From

Ad Spend Data

Google Ads Transparency Center

Google publishes political advertising data as part of its transparency requirements. This dataset covers every political ad run on Google platforms in the U.S., including advertiser name, state, date range, spend range, and targeting parameters like age and gender.

We downloaded records covering the 2020 and 2024 election periods, cleaned state codes, filled null targeting fields with "Broad Targeting," and built a composite join key from state and year.

Ads_final.csv
adstransparency.google.com
Socioeconomic Panel

U.S. Census Bureau (ACS)

The American Community Survey is an annual Census Bureau survey collecting detailed social and economic data for every U.S. state. We pulled four tables for each year from 2020 to 2024 and cleaned them with automated Python functions before combining into a single panel.

S1901 (median household income), S1701 (poverty rate), S1501 (bachelor's degree attainment), DP05 (total and voting-age population).

master_socioeconomic_panel.csv
data.census.gov
Political Outcomes

Wikipedia

Used to label each state as Democratic or Republican for the 2020 and 2024 election cycles, drawing from U.S. presidential and Senate election results pages. This allowed the Tableau dashboards to color-code spending by party outcome and identify states that flipped between cycles.

States were classified based on the presidential race result for each cycle, with battleground states flagged separately for the swing state analysis.

political_leaning_by_election.csv

04 / Pipeline

How We Built the Dataset

Step 01

Census Extraction

Downloaded ACS 5-year estimates for income (S1901), poverty (S1701), education (S1501), and population (DP05) for each year 2020 to 2024.

ACSST*Y*.S1901*.csv
Step 02

Per-Indicator Cleaning

Automated Python functions extracted the target row per indicator, cleaned Census symbols (N, X, -), and stripped commas and percent signs from numeric fields.

clean_census_income_auto()
Step 03

Panel Assembly

Four yearly panels were merged on State and Year. Full state names were mapped to 2-letter abbreviations using a lookup CSV before saving the final panel.

master_socioeconomic_panel.csv
Step 04

Bridge Key Join

A composite State_Year_Key (e.g. "FL2024") joined the Google Ads data to the socioeconomic panel. A null audit confirmed no unmatched rows after DC name normalization.

state_code + year
Step 05

Tableau Dashboards

The final merged dataset was loaded into Tableau for choropleth maps, scatter plots, and dual-axis bar charts across four interactive dashboards.

Conclusion

Electoral importance, not demographics, drives where Google political ad dollars go. Swing states are the gravity wells of modern digital campaign spending.

Socioeconomic indicators associate moderately with Democratic-leaning states in the aggregate, but within swing states where the money actually flows, demographic profiles are too mixed to serve as targeting signals. The better predictor of ad spend is simple: will this state be contested on election day?
